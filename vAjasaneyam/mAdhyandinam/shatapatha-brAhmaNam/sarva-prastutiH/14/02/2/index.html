Mahīdhara takes it in the sense of ‘having their barhis pointed upwards,’ i.e. towards the east (!), the peculiar feature of the barhis in the present case–as far as the participation of the Fathers in the drinking of the Gharma is concerned–being its having the tops of the grass-stalks turned to the east instead of to the south, as is the case in all ceremonies relating to the Fathers. The term ‘ūrdhvabarhis’ might possibly mean ‘having their (special) barhis above,’ i.e. in the world of the Fathers, where they would be supposed to partake of the libations of hot milk; whilst yet another (suggested by the next paragraph) would be that of ‘having the barhis above them;’ which would, however, be more appropriate if the secreting of the stick under the barhis applied to the present, instead of the next one. ↩︎
